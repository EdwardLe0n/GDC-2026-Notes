## Lesson Three:

***Trailer and Campaign over Product***

> **Must do:** Treat your trailer and campaign page like a major release.
> 
> Tell your story, *not just your features*

### Remember these three main points...

1. Backers aren't just buying a product. They're deciding whether to believe in you
2. Prioritize video and GIFs over screenshots. Backers convert on motion
3. Plan your Stretch Goals! Make the first few reachable, then let the gaps grow

### Some Big Story Points Include:

1. Honesty about your stage in development
2. Why Kickstarter? Why Now?
3. Ask players to join us. We couldn't have gotten here without there support!

### The Extra Notes 

*(same thing as [this tbh](#remember-these-three-main-points))*

- Gifs and videos should be used heavily
- Stretch goals should grow in value and cost (how much the customers give to campaign)
  - Cosmetics early on versus levels later on

## Lesson 4:

***Tier Architecture Matters More Than You Think!***

- Avoid physical releases (as much as possible)
  - Shipping and fulfillment was expensive and chaos
  - Cannot stress this enough. For most campaigns, this was just not worth it

### Couple of Sweet Spots for Tiers

|Price|---|
|---|---|
|$30|Entry|
|$60|Core|
|$100|Popular|
|$450|Premium|
|$10,000|Top Tier|

> The project by Playable Worlds, Inc had an average pledge of $137!

**Also:** Avoid having too many tiers (for instance, 25 may be too many...)

### Some more notes on tiers...

**Early Birds:**

- They matter, and will sell out fast
- Make different batches for different time zones!

**As for rewards vs. tiers...**

- Make the rewards first!

> Wasn't able to save the thought in my notes, but I believe the end of that thought was that you can easily connect tiers to rewards once the rewards are made!
>
> **Just make rewards first, trust**

## Lesson 5

***The Middle Will Kill You***

- Kickstarter only notifies people during the first & least point!

> You need to plan for what happens between the Kickstarter before opening it!
